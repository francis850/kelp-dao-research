The defense · 42 min 36 sec after the drain

blockUserTransfers lands. Ten addresses frozen in one transaction.

An LRT manager at Kelp calls blockUserTransfers on the rsETH implementation contract. Ten addresses enter a 24-hour transfer block: the seven named cash-out EOAs, plus three look-alike decoys that share leading hex with the real ones (0xeba73b81… next to 0xeba786c9…; 0x1f4cf71e… next to 0x1f4c1c2e…; 0xcbb25ef4… next to 0xcbb24a6b…). Someone on the Kelp side anticipated typo-squat laundering and pre-empted it.

The block is time-boxed. transfersBlockedUntil[account] is set to block.timestamp + 1 days for each. If the block is not refreshed, it expires. Every transfer to or from a blocked address now reverts with TransfersBlocked(address, uint256), selector 0x5f4854f9.

Secondary defense

Kelp's emergency multisig calls pause().

This is the "46-minute freeze" credited across press coverage with blocking further drains. It is a different function from blockUserTransfers, executed two minutes and forty-nine seconds later. Both defenses sit in the same contract. The press conflated them. The block is what stopped the retries.

07:15:59 Sun · Apr 19 block 24912376
The clawback · 13h 40m after the drain

Kelp recovers 40,373.72 rsETH from the OFT adapter to custody.

A Kelp admin calls recoverFrozenFunds(0x85d456b2…) on the rsETH implementation. The function sweeps the entire rsETH balance of the blocked adapter to a pre-configured custody address: 0xb3696a817d01c8623e66d156b6798291fa10a46d. Quote from RSETH.sol line 202: "Bypass transfer block enforcement when transferring to custody address." The block mechanism exists to enable the clawback, not just to buy time.

Forty thousand three hundred seventy-three rsETH, worth approximately $101.8 million at the pre-drain oracle price. Thirty-four point seven percent of the stolen supply, reclaimed in a single transaction. Combined with the $71 million that the Arbitrum Security Council will freeze two days later, total recovery hits fifty-nine percent.

This event is public. The FrozenFundsRecovered(from, to, amount) log is indexable on any Ethereum node. Every forensic report this piece compared against, from QuillAudits to Innora to DeFiPrime to Kelp's own Aave governance post, passed over it. The contract was designed for exactly this case. The case arrived. The function executed. No headline noticed.

The strongest defensive moment of the attack was not a pause and not a bridge shutdown. It was a function call four hundred ten lines deep in the token source, from a role key held by one Kelp admin, that returned forty thousand tokens to the issuer before anyone wrote about it.

Kelp refreshes the blocks on a rolling twenty-four-hour schedule.

Closing note · what the record says

Three misattributions live in the public coverage.

The "46-minute multisig freeze" is credited with blocking further drains. It was pause(), three minutes after the real defense, and it stops new mints, not transfers from existing addresses.

LayerZero's DVN shutdown is credited with stopping the retries. The retries reverted at the rsETH token, not at the Endpoint. Zero logs on both retry transactions confirm the revert sits upstream of any cross-chain message handling.

The "additional drains blocked" is framed as pause-derived. The retries blocked because blockUserTransfers had already added the attacker to the token-level block list three minutes earlier.

None of this is secret. All of it is on chain. The public record has not yet caught up.
